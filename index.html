About This Episode

Concrete is the most widely used construction material on Earth — and one of the most polluting. In this episode, Mahshid and Yara take a deep dive into the environmental cost of concrete production, exploring how the cement industry accounts for roughly 8% of global CO₂ emissions each year.

But it's not all grim news. From cutting-edge recycling techniques to carbon-capturing "green" concrete mixes, there are real and scalable solutions emerging worldwide. Together, we unpack the science, the policy landscape, and the innovators who are quietly remaking one of humanity's oldest industries.

Whether you're a student of engineering, an environmentally curious listener, or simply someone who walks past construction sites and wonders about the grey blocks going up — this episode is for you.

🌍

Why This Topic Matters Globally

Cement production alone releases more CO₂ than the entire aviation industry. As urbanisation accelerates — especially across Asia and Africa — concrete demand is projected to grow by 50% by 2050. Without urgent innovation in how we make and reuse concrete, meeting the Paris Agreement targets will be nearly impossible. Sustainable concrete is not a niche topic; it is a civilisational necessity.

Key Topics

  • Environmental impact of concrete

    How Portland cement production drives large-scale carbon emissions and why the construction sector is under increasing regulatory pressure.

  • Concrete recycling

    Techniques for crushing, sorting, and repurposing demolished concrete as recycled aggregate — reducing landfill waste and virgin material demand.

  • Green concrete

    Alternative binders such as fly ash, ground granulated blast-furnace slag (GGBS), and geopolymers that cut cement content dramatically.

  • Sustainable technologies

    Carbon capture and mineralisation during curing, 3D-printed concrete for less material waste, and bio-inspired self-healing concrete mixes.

Transcript Partial

Mahshid

Welcome back to EcoCast. I'm Mahshid, and today I'm joined as always by my wonderful co-host Yara. Today we're talking about something that literally surrounds us every single day — concrete. And I think most people would be shocked if they knew how much of a climate impact it has.

Yara

Exactly. I mean, we walk past grey buildings and grey roads and we just don't think about it. But the numbers are staggering. Cement — which is the binding agent in concrete — is responsible for around eight percent of all global CO₂ emissions. To put that in perspective, that's more than the entire aviation sector.

Mahshid

And the demand is only going up. Rapid urbanisation in countries like India, China, and across sub-Saharan Africa means we're going to need a lot more infrastructure in the coming decades. So the question really is: can we keep building without destroying the planet?

Yara

That's the core tension of this episode. And the answer isn't a simple yes or no. There are genuinely exciting developments in what's called green concrete — where you replace a portion of the cement with industrial by-products like fly ash from coal plants or slag from steel manufacturing. These are materials that would otherwise go to waste, and they can cut the carbon footprint of concrete by up to forty or even fifty percent.

Mahshid

I love that. It's almost a circular economy story — turning one industry's waste into another industry's resource. But I also want to talk about concrete recycling, because I think that's often overlooked. What happens to all the demolished concrete when a building comes down?

Yara

Traditionally, most of it ends up in landfill, or at best gets crushed and used as low-grade fill material under roads. But there are newer techniques — high-quality separation and processing — that allow recycled concrete aggregate to be used in structural applications. It's not mainstream yet, but the technology is there.

Mahshid

And then there's the really futuristic stuff — carbon mineralisation, where CO₂ is actually injected into fresh concrete as it cures and gets permanently locked into the material. You're literally storing carbon inside your buildings. I find that concept almost poetic.

Yara

It really is. Companies like CarbonCure are already doing this at commercial scale. And then there's geopolymer concrete, which eliminates Portland cement entirely and replaces it with alkali-activated materials. The structural performance is comparable, but the carbon emissions can be reduced by up to eighty percent.

Mahshid

So we have the solutions. The question is always adoption — policy, industry standards, procurement rules. We'll dig into that more in the second half of the episode. But first, let's take a short break.
